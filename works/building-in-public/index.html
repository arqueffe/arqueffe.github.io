Building in Public: Why I Share Everything

  • career
  • opensource
  • learning
  • community

The Scary Decision

A year ago, I made a terrifying commitment: share everything publicly. Every project, every failure, every lesson learned. No cherry-picking wins, no hiding struggles.

It changed everything.

What “Building in Public” Means

It’s not just about posting progress updates. It’s about complete transparency:

  • Sharing code from day one (even when it’s ugly)
  • Documenting failures as thoroughly as successes
  • Live-streaming debugging sessions
  • Publishing revenue numbers
  • Admitting when you have no idea what you’re doing

Why I Started

The Impostor Syndrome Trap

For years, I curated a perfect professional image. Every commit polished, every project spotlight-ready. And I felt like a fraud.

The gap between my private chaos and public perfection was crushing.

The Epiphany

I realized: everyone feels this way. The seniors I admired? They googled basic syntax. The “experts” I followed? They shipped bugs and rewrote code constantly.

Perfect is a lie. Progress is messy.

What Changed

1. Better Code Through Accountability

Knowing people are watching makes you:

  • Write clearer documentation (for future you and others)
  • Make deliberate architectural decisions
  • Actually finish projects instead of hoarding prototypes

2. Unexpected Collaborations

My messy-but-public projects attracted:

  • Contributors who saw potential I missed
  • Mentors who offered guidance
  • Companies interested in my problem-solving approach

“That half-broken side project got me my dream job. Not my polished portfolio.”

3. Learning Accelerated

When you teach, you learn twice:

  • Explaining forces deep understanding
  • Questions expose knowledge gaps
  • Different perspectives challenge assumptions

4. Community Over Competition

The shift from “protect your ideas” to “share everything” was liberating. Ideas are cheap‚Äîexecution is everything. And execution improves with collaboration.

The Uncomfortable Truth

Not everything went well. Building in public means:

Public Failures

That time I spent 3 months on a project nobody wanted? Documented. The architectural decision I completely reversed? Explained in detail.

The result? More valuable than any success story. People reached out saying, “I made the same mistake” or “You just saved me months.”

Dealing with Criticism

Early on, someone commented: “This code is terrible.”

My first instinct? Defensive rage.

But they were right. And they explained why. And my code got better.

Lesson: Criticism in public is peer review you didn’t have to pay for.

Time Investment

Building in public takes time:

  • Writing docs
  • Recording videos
  • Answering questions

But it compounds. Your documentation becomes your marketing. Your tutorials become your network.

Practical Tips

Start Small

You don’t need to live-stream your entire life:

  1. Share one work-in-progress project
  2. Write a monthly update
  3. Post one lesson learned per week

Be Authentic

Skip the corporate speak. Share:

  • What actually happened
  • How you actually felt
  • What you’d do differently

Embrace Imperfection

Your first blog post will be rough. Your first video will be awkward. Your first open-source project will have bugs.

Post them anyway.

Choose Your Platform

  • GitHub for code
  • Twitter/Mastodon for updates
  • Blog for deep dives
  • YouTube for visual learners

Pick one. Master it. Expand later.

Your Turn

You don’t need permission. You don’t need to be an expert. You just need to start.

Challenge: Share one thing this week. One project, one lesson, one struggle. See what happens.

The scary part? People might see.

The beautiful part? People might see.

A year ago, I was scared to share. Today, I can’t imagine building any other way.
